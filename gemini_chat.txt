Do aqara products require an aqara hub to be used in homeassistant (HA)? Or just zigbee and or thread/matter in HA
The short answer is: No, you do not need an Aqara Hub for most products, provided you have the right radio hardware for Home Assistant.
Aqara products primarily communicate using Zigbee or Thread, and Home Assistant is perfectly capable of speaking those "languages" directly.
1. Using Zigbee (Most Aqara Sensors)
If you have an Aqara Door/Window sensor, Motion sensor, or Temperature sensor, these are almost certainly Zigbee devices. 
Reddit
•	Requirements: You need a Zigbee USB dongle (like the Home Assistant SkyConnect, Sonoff ZBDongle-E, or SLZB-06) plugged into your HA machine. 
Reddit
•	How it works: You use the ZHA (Zigbee Home Automation) or Zigbee2MQTT integration. You put the Aqara device in pairing mode, and HA will find it directly. 
Reddit+ 1
•	Caveat: Some older Aqara Zigbee devices are "picky" about their Zigbee mesh. They don't always follow the Zigbee standard perfectly and may drop off the network if they try to route through certain non-Aqara smart plugs (like some older Centralite or Ledvance plugs).
2. Using Thread / Matter (Newer P2/U200 Line)
Aqara’s newer "P2" sensors and the "U200" lock use Matter-over-Thread.
•	Requirements: You need a Thread Border Router. This can be your Home Assistant machine (using a SkyConnect dongle flashed with multi-protocol firmware) or a 3rd party device like an Apple HomePod Mini, Google Nest Hub (Gen 2), or an Amazon Echo (4th Gen). 
Reddit
•	How it works: You add the device via the Matter integration in Home Assistant. 
Reddit
3. When you might actually want the Hub
While not required, there are two specific reasons some users keep the hub:
•	Firmware Updates: It is much easier to update Aqara device firmware via their own hub. While Zigbee2MQTT and ZHA support "Over-the-Air" (OTA) updates, Aqara is often slow to release those files to the public community.
•	Advanced Features: Some complex devices (like the FP2 Presence Sensor which is Wi-Fi based, or specific light strips with complex effects) sometimes have configuration options that are only exposed in the Aqara Home app.

The Problem: "Potential-Free" vs. "Mains Voltage"
•	Your Qubinos (and Shelly): These use Mains Voltage (230V) sensing. You send 230V down your switch wire, and when it comes back to the "I1" terminal, the device triggers.
•	Sonoff MINI DUO: The S1 and S2 terminals are Potential-Free (Dry Contact) inputs. They provide their own tiny internal voltage (usually 3.3V DC). They only want to see a physical connection (loop) between S1 and S2.
•	The Danger: If you wire your loft so that 230V (Mains) is sent down to the wall switch and returned to the S1/S2 ports, the Sonoff will literally explode/fry instantly.
Your 3 Solutions

Would you like me to show you how to set a "Presence Timeout" in Home Assistant so the lights don't flicker off the moment you hold your breath for a second?
My primary use case would be to turn the room lights off at say 11:00pm, but not if the room was still occupied, but to switch off once unoccupied
This is a classic Home Assistant "Presence-Aware" logic problem. In a bungalow, this is great for ensuring you aren't plunged into darkness while finishing a late-night film or reading.
The most robust way to do this in a single automation—without messy "wait" timers that can fail if HA reboots—is using Trigger IDs.
The Logic
You want the lights to turn off if:
1.	It is 11:00 PM AND the room is Clear.
2.	OR it is After 11:00 PM and the room becomes Clear.
The Automation Code (YAML)
You can paste this into the YAML editor of a new automation. Just replace the entity names with your actual ones.
YAML
alias: "Late Night Light Off - Presence Aware"
description: "Turn off lights at 11pm, or as soon as room is empty after 11pm"
mode: restart
trigger:
  - platform: time
    at: "23:00:00"
    id: "time_trigger"
  - platform: state
    entity_id: binary_sensor.aqara_fp1e_presence  # Your Presence Sensor
    to: "off"
    id: "presence_cleared"
condition:
  # This ensures the presence_cleared trigger only works late at night
  - condition: time
    after: "22:59:59"
    before: "05:00:00"
action:
  - if:
      - condition: state
        entity_id: binary_sensor.aqara_fp1e_presence
        state: "off"
    then:
      - service: light.turn_off
        target:
          entity_id: light.living_room_lights  # Your Aqara T2 Relay
Why this works:
•	The 11:00 PM Trigger: If the clock hits 11:00 PM, the automation checks if the sensor is "off." If you are in the room, the if condition fails, and nothing happens. (The lights stay on).
•	The "Presence Cleared" Trigger: If you leave the room at 11:15 PM, the sensor changes to "off." This triggers the automation again. Since it’s after 11:00 PM (Condition) and the sensor is now "off" (If-Action), the lights shut down.
•	The Mode: Restart: This is important for mmWave sensors. If you walk out and walk back in quickly, it "resets" the logic.
Pro-Tip for your Aqara T2 & FP1E
Since you are using the Aqara T2 relay, I recommend adding a 1-minute delay to the "Presence Cleared" trigger inside the trigger block. mmWave sensors are so sensitive that they can sometimes flicker "off" for a split second if you sit perfectly still or a pet moves; adding a small "for: 00:01:00" buffer prevents the lights from flickering off while you're still there.

1. How it works without a Neutral
A smart switch needs a tiny amount of power to keep its Zigbee radio "alive" so it can hear your Home Assistant commands.
•	With a Neutral: It takes power from Live and Neutral.

In the aqara t2 dual relay, what is meant by dry/wet contact
In the context of the Aqara T2 Dual Relay, "Dry" and "Wet" contact modes refer to how the relay output handles electrical power. This is the "killer feature" of the T2, as it allows you to control things that require their own separate power or just a simple button-press signal.
1. Wet Contact Mode (Powered)
In Wet Contact mode, the relay "leaks" the power used to run the module into the output terminals.
•	How it works: You use the included red jumper wire to connect the LIN and LOUT terminals. This bridges the incoming mains power () directly to the relay switches.
•	The Result: When you turn the relay ON, it sends directly to whatever is connected.
•	Common Use Case: Standard light bulbs, fans, or any appliance that doesn't have its own power source.
2. Dry Contact Mode (Potential-Free)
In Dry Contact mode, the relay acts like a "dumb" physical switch that is completely isolated from the module's power.
•	How it works: You remove the red jumper wire. The L and N terminals power the Aqara brain, but the LOUT, L1, and L2 terminals are now just "isolated gates."
•	The Result: Turning the relay ON simply "clicks" the internal connection shut. No power is sent from the Aqara module to the load.
•	Common Use Case:
o	Garage Doors: Most garage openers just need a "pulse" of two wires touching (a button press). Sending would fry the circuit board.
o	Boilers: Many UK boilers have "T1/T2" terminals for a thermostat that only want a dry contact.
o	Different Voltages: You can power the Aqara module with AC, but use the "dry" relay to switch a or DC circuit (like an electric gate or LED strip).
Summary Table
Feature	Wet Contact	Dry Contact
Red Jumper	Installed (LIN to LOUT)	Removed
Output Voltage	Matches Input ( AC)	Zero (Isolated)
Wiring Logic	Relay supplies the power	Relay completes an existing circuit
Best For	Lights, Fans, Sockets	Garage Doors, Boilers, Low Voltage
